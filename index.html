Restructuring Timeline

How Spirit’s capital and operations evolved across multiple restructuring phases.

  1. Nov 2024 Initial Chapter 11 filing

    Spirit files for Chapter 11 and begins its first balance-sheet restructuring, targeting deleveraging and new capital after the failed JetBlue transaction.

  2. Dec 2024 – Mar 2025 Rights offering and emergence

    Spirit conducts an equity rights offering, restructures funded debt, and issues new secured notes as it emerges from its initial restructuring with a revised capital structure.

  3. Mid 2025 Persistent losses and liquidity pressure

    Despite the first restructuring, macro and operational headwinds continue. Management discloses ongoing losses and pressure on liquidity and leverage.

  4. Aug 2025 Second Chapter 11

    A second filing follows. The new case focuses on a deeper reset: a creditor-led DIP facility, roll-up of prepetition notes, and a more significant fleet and cost transformation.

  5. Autumn 2025 DIP approval and roll-up elections

    The court approves the DIP credit agreement. Existing secured noteholders are invited to participate, providing new money and rolling part of their prepetition exposure into superpriority DIP claims.

  6. Ongoing Fleet, lease, and labor actions

    Spirit cuts capacity, negotiates with lessors, exits weaker routes, and adjusts staffing as it reshapes the airline around a smaller, more focused network and multi-year recovery plan.

Capital Structure & DIP Overview

How existing bondholders moved from traditional secured creditors to DIP lenders with superpriority claims.

Key elements

  • A multi-tranche DIP term loan provides liquidity during the case.
  • Existing secured noteholders supply the DIP and receive the ability to roll up part of their prepetition exposure into DIP claims.
  • The capital structure shifts from emergent, equity-supported funding to a more classic creditor-owned turnaround model.
  • Legacy equity sits at the bottom of the waterfall and is likely wiped or heavily diluted in any confirmed plan.

For corporate actions teams, the most visible mechanics are the DIP participation and roll-up elections that appear as complex voluntary events with strict eligibility criteria, deadlines, and allocation logic.
