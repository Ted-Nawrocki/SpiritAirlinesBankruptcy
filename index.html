Restructuring Timeline

How Spirit’s capital and operations evolved across multiple restructuring phases.

  1. Nov 2024 Initial Chapter 11 filing

    Spirit files for Chapter 11 and begins its first balance-sheet restructuring, targeting deleveraging and new capital after the failed JetBlue transaction.

  2. Mar 2025 Emergence and new notes

    Spirit emerges with new secured notes and equity raised through a rights offer. The airline returns to the market with a lighter capital structure but fragile fundamentals.

  3. Aug 2025 Second Chapter 11

    A second filing follows ongoing losses and liquidity pressure. The new case introduces a creditor-led plan anchored by a DIP facility and a roll-up of prepetition notes.

  4. Autumn 2025 DIP approval and roll-up elections

    The court approves the DIP facility, and existing secured noteholders are invited to participate, providing new money and rolling part of their prepetition exposure into superpriority DIP claims.

  5. Ongoing Fleet, lease, and labor actions

    Spirit cuts capacity, negotiates with lessors, exits weaker routes, and pursues cost reductions as it reshapes the airline around a smaller, more focused network.

Capital Structure & DIP Overview

How existing bondholders moved from traditional secured creditors to DIP lenders with superpriority claims.

Key elements

  • A multi-tranche DIP term loan provides liquidity during the case.
  • Existing secured noteholders supply the DIP and receive the ability to roll up part of their prepetition exposure into DIP claims.
  • The capital structure shifts from “emergence-focused” to a more classic creditor-owned turnaround model.
  • Legacy equity sits at the bottom of the waterfall and is likely wiped or heavily diluted in any confirmed plan.

For corporate actions teams, the most visible mechanics are the DIP participation and roll-up elections that appear as complex voluntary events with strict eligibility criteria, deadlines, and allocation logic.

Fleet, Network, and Operational Changes

Bankruptcy is not only a capital structure tool. Spirit is also reshaping its fleet, routes, and cost base in parallel with the financial restructuring.

What is changing

  • Negotiations with lessors, including a high-profile settlement with a major leasing partner, support lease rejections and aircraft returns.
  • Capacity is cut as unprofitable routes are exited and the focus shifts toward stronger markets.
  • Labor and staffing levels are adjusted to align with a smaller fleet and network footprint.
  • Management’s projections rely on a smaller, leaner airline generating improved margins over a multi-year horizon.
