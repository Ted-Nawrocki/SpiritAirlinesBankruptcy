Breaking Developments – April 2026

Last updated Apr. 22, 2026

The situation at Spirit has escalated materially in the past week. A fuel price shock roughly doubled the cost assumptions underpinning the "Project Soar" restructuring plan, creditors filed objections, liquidation risk surged, and the Trump administration is now in advanced talks on a reported $500 million rescue package that could give the U.S. government up to 90% of the reorganized airline. A court disclosure statement hearing is scheduled for April 23, 2026. The situation remains fluid. All information should be verified against primary sources before use.

The fuel shock

  • Spirit's "Project Soar" restructuring plan was built on jet fuel averaging approximately $2.24 per gallon in 2026 and $2.14 per gallon in 2027, per the airline's March 2026 plan disclosures as reported by Reuters.
  • By mid-April, jet fuel prices were approximately $4.24 per gallon -- roughly double the plan's assumptions -- driven in part by geopolitical tensions stemming from the U.S.-Iran conflict.
  • J.P. Morgan analysts estimated the fuel spike could swing Spirit's projected 2026 operating margin from the plan's assumed +0.5% to approximately negative 20%, adding roughly $360 million in incremental annual costs -- a figure exceeding the airline's year-end unrestricted cash balance of approximately $273 million (Reuters, citing Spirit's end-of-2025 disclosures).
  • Under "Project Soar," Spirit projected its cash balance would fall to approximately $87 million at its lowest point. The fuel shock eliminated the margin that projection assumed.

Court objections and hearing

  • Citibank, as administrative agent for Spirit's revolving credit facility lenders, filed a court objection arguing that the plan's financial projections assume fuel prices ease later in the year and do not model a sustained high-cost scenario. Citibank also indicated Spirit may already be in default on certain credit agreement provisions, potentially requiring repayment of more than $35 million or additional collateral posting (Reuters).
  • The U.S. Trustee's Office filed a separate objection to Spirit's disclosure statement on April 14, 2026, arguing it is too thin and does not adequately explain why reorganization was chosen over alternatives, including a potential sale of the business (Law360 / Reuters).
  • The primary disclosure statement hearing before Judge Sean Lane at the U.S. Bankruptcy Court for the Southern District of New York is scheduled for April 23, 2026. Approval of the disclosure statement is a prerequisite to soliciting creditor votes on the plan.
  • Sources cited by Bloomberg and CNBC indicated a liquidation decision could come within days as of mid-April. Reuters separately reported that liquidation was not the most likely near-term outcome and that Spirit was "shaking all the trees" to raise cash. Spirit's official position: "We don't comment on market rumors and speculation" (Spirit to CNBC, April 2026).

$500M government rescue -- in advanced talks

  • Bloomberg, CNBC, NBC News, CNN, and the Wall Street Journal reported on April 22, 2026 that the Trump administration is in advanced talks on a rescue package of up to $500 million for Spirit. The deal, if finalized, would provide emergency liquidity and give the U.S. government warrants to acquire up to 90% of the reorganized carrier upon emergence from bankruptcy.
  • The financing would rank as senior debt, placing the government ahead of existing stakeholders. A White House official confirmed details are still being negotiated and could change. CNN reported a deal could be announced as soon as Wednesday night or Thursday (April 23-24).
  • President Trump signaled support on CNBC's Squawk Box on April 22: "Spirit's in trouble, and I'd love somebody to buy Spirit. It's 14,000 jobs, and maybe the federal government should help that one out."
  • Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy offered a notably cautious view in a Reuters interview the same day: "What we don't want to do is put good money after bad, and there's been a lot of money thrown at Spirit, and they haven't found their way into profitability. And so would we just forestall the inevitable and then own that?"
  • FAA Administrator Bryan Bedford interjected publicly that the agency's funds should not be used. Senator Ted Cruz (R-TX), chair of the Senate Commerce Committee, wrote on April 22 that a government rescue would be "an absolutely TERRIBLE idea." United Airlines CEO Scott Kirby also publicly opposed it. Fitch Ratings senior director Joe Rohlena said Spirit would face "a difficult path even with a government rescue."
  • The Trump administration has previously taken equity stakes in companies it viewed as nationally significant, including Intel and USA Rare Earth, though Spirit would represent an unusual case given its active bankruptcy status (CNBC).

Corporate actions lens

  • If a government rescue closes, the waterfall changes: new senior government financing would sit above existing DIP claims in a revised priority structure. Monitor for amended plan filings reflecting new capital terms.
  • If the case converts to Chapter 7, corporate actions mechanics shift entirely: assets are liquidated, proceeds distributed per the absolute priority rule, and no new equity is issued under a plan of reorganization.
  • Verify all positions against internal risk, legal, and compliance teams before acting. No deal has been confirmed as of April 22, 2026.

Restructuring Timeline

How Spirit's capital structure and operations evolved across two Chapter 11 cases.

  1. Nov. 18, 2024 First Chapter 11 filing

    Spirit Aviation Holdings files for Chapter 11 in the U.S. Bankruptcy Court for the Southern District of New York (Case No. 24-11988). The filing followed the DOJ's January 2024 block of the $3.8 billion JetBlue merger, eliminating management's primary strategic alternative for addressing approximately $3.3 billion of debt. Spirit had accumulated more than $2.5 billion in losses since 2020.

  2. Feb. 20 – Mar. 12, 2025 Plan confirmation and emergence

    The court confirms Spirit's Plan of Reorganization on February 20, 2025. The first case was a prepackaged restructuring: approximately $795 million of funded debt was equitized, and Spirit received a $350 million equity investment from existing investors. Spirit emerges March 12, 2025 with Ted Christie continuing as President and CEO. The case ran 114 days.

  3. Mar. – Aug. 2025 Persistent losses and liquidity deterioration

    Despite the first restructuring, the airline faces softer domestic leisure demand, continued high costs, and Pratt & Whitney engine reliability issues grounding a portion of its Airbus A320neo fleet. Spirit had forecast a $252 million net profit for 2025; instead it reported a net loss of approximately $257 million in the period March 13 through June 30, 2025. As of June 30, 2025, cash stood at $407.5 million. Spirit drew the full $275 million available under its revolving credit facility in August 2025. AerCap, its largest aircraft lessor, terminates leases in July 2025 following an alleged default, accelerating the path to a second filing.

  4. Aug. 29, 2025 Second Chapter 11 filing

    Spirit Aviation Holdings files for Chapter 11 a second time in the same court, five months after emerging from the first case. This is colloquially termed a "Chapter 22" in financial and restructuring circles. Unlike the first case, which focused narrowly on balance-sheet deleveraging, the second filing addresses fleet size, network structure, and cost base simultaneously. Dave Davis serves as President and CEO. The airline continues operating with tickets, credits, and loyalty points honored.

  5. Oct. 10, 2025 DIP financing and AerCap settlement approved

    The U.S. Bankruptcy Court approves a $475 million multi-tranche DIP facility provided by Spirit's existing bondholders. $200 million is immediately available. Separately, the court approves Spirit's settlement with AerCap Ireland Limited: AerCap makes a $150 million payment to Spirit, 27 aircraft leases are rejected, all claims between the parties are resolved, and the agreement provides for future delivery of 30 aircraft. Spirit notes the settlement reduces operating costs by hundreds of millions of dollars.

  6. Nov. 2025 – Feb. 2026 Fleet reduction and operational restructuring

    Spirit negotiates lease rejections for 58 aircraft, exits service in 11 U.S. cities, and reduces staffing including approximately 270 pilot furloughs effective November 1 and furloughs of approximately 1,800 flight attendants by December 1. The fleet shrinks from 214 aircraft at the time of filing to 114 through prior lease rejections and retirements. Spirit requests a 120-day extension of its plan filing deadline in November 2025, citing the scope of the operational restructuring underway.

  7. Mar. 13, 2026 RSA and Plan of Reorganization filed

    Spirit files a Restructuring Support Agreement and Plan of Reorganization with the court, backed by its DIP lenders and secured noteholders. The plan targets further fleet reduction to 76–80 aircraft (primarily A320/A321ceo) by Q3 2026. Total debt and lease obligations are targeted to fall from $7.4 billion to approximately $2 billion at exit. Plan confirmation is targeted for May or June 2026, with emergence by early summer 2026. An auction for approximately 20 aircraft was also announced, with CSDS Asset Management as stalking-horse bidder.

  8. Apr. 16–22, 2026 Fuel shock, liquidation risk, and $500M government rescue talks

    Bloomberg, Reuters, and CNBC report on April 16 that a jet fuel surge has rendered "Project Soar" financially precarious, with creditors weighing liquidation. By April 22, Bloomberg, CNBC, NBC News, CNN, and the Wall Street Journal report that the Trump administration is in advanced talks on a $500 million rescue package that would give the U.S. government warrants to acquire up to 90% of the reorganized airline. President Trump publicly endorses exploring the deal; Transportation Secretary Duffy expresses skepticism. A disclosure statement hearing is scheduled before the bankruptcy court on April 23, 2026. No deal has been confirmed as of April 22, 2026.

  9. Pending Plan confirmation and emergence -- or restructured path

    The original Plan of Reorganization targeted confirmation in May or June 2026 and emergence by early summer. That timeline is now under pressure. Potential outcomes include: a revised plan reflecting new fuel assumptions, a sale of the business, conversion to Chapter 7 liquidation, or new financing that allows the existing plan to proceed. Corporate actions teams should monitor court filings closely.

Capital Structure & DIP Overview

How existing bondholders moved from traditional secured creditors to DIP lenders with superpriority claims, and the balance-sheet targets embedded in the current Plan of Reorganization.

Key elements

  • A multi-tranche DIP term loan of up to $475 million was approved by the court on October 10, 2025. $200 million was immediately available upon approval.
  • Existing bondholders supply the DIP and retain the ability to roll up a portion of their prepetition secured exposure into superpriority DIP claims.
  • The first case converted $795 million of funded debt to equity and raised $350 million of new equity. That deleveraging proved insufficient to offset continued operating losses.
  • The Plan of Reorganization filed March 13, 2026 targets reducing total debt and lease obligations from $7.4 billion to approximately $2 billion at exit.
  • Legacy equity from the first restructuring sits at the bottom of the waterfall; the plan reflects a creditor-owned exit.

For corporate actions teams, the most visible mechanics remain the DIP participation and roll-up elections, which appear as complex voluntary events with strict eligibility criteria, deadlines, and allocation logic. As the plan moves toward confirmation, watch for distribution-related events tied to new equity issuance and any reinstated or renegotiated debt instruments.

Fleet, Network, and Operational Changes

The second case is primarily an operational restructuring. Spirit is shrinking its fleet to approximately one-third of its pre-bankruptcy size, exiting unprofitable markets, and reducing headcount in parallel with the financial restructuring.

What is changing

  • AerCap settlement (court-approved Oct. 10, 2025): AerCap pays Spirit $150 million, 27 aircraft leases are rejected, and all claims between the parties are resolved. Separately, 30 future aircraft deliveries are retained.
  • Spirit has negotiated the rejection of 58 aircraft leases in addition to the AerCap settlement, reducing the fleet from 214 at filing to 114 as of early 2026.
  • The Plan of Reorganization targets a final fleet of 76–80 aircraft (primarily Airbus A320/A321ceo) by Q3 2026. An auction for approximately 20 additional aircraft was announced in March 2026, with CSDS Asset Management as stalking-horse bidder at a $530 million floor price.
  • Spirit exited service in 11 U.S. cities beginning October 2025, including Albuquerque, Birmingham, Boise, Chattanooga, Columbia, Oakland, Sacramento, San Jose, San Diego, Portland, and Salt Lake City.
  • Workforce reductions include approximately 270 pilot furloughs effective November 1, 2025, downgrading of 140 captains to first officers, and furloughs of approximately 1,800 flight attendants by December 1, 2025 (approximately one-third of cabin crew).
  • Engine reliability issues with Pratt & Whitney-powered A320neo aircraft grounded a portion of the fleet in 2025, compounding capacity constraints.
